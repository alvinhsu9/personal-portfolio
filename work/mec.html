A case study on Mountain Equipment CO-OP

* Read disclaimer notice here *

Overview

The purpose of the case study was to determine key functionality/usability problems on the website. Through our research and usability tests, we were able to determine issues on their site. Based on the results we collected, we propose solutions for how their issues can be addressed.

Research

Heuristics analysis

A heuristic analysis is an evaluation of user interface design using usability principles that determines what should be the best practices for web design.


Once we applied the evaluation to the MEC website, we found that the lowest scores were ‘Forms and Data Entry’ and ‘Search’, while the highest scores were ‘Trust and Credibility’ and ‘Page Layout and Visual Design’. Our results imply where users would have the most trouble with on the website, and where they would have the most positive feedback.

Archetypes

First we developed a list of archetypes who represent those who would potentially use and benefit from the website. This list was created to help us better understand how these users interact with the website. Ideally, we would want to recruit participants that fit into these archetypes.

  • Hikers
  • Outdoor Enthusiasts
  • Snowboards & Skiiers
  • Rock climbers/boulderers
  • Travel enthusiasts
  • Camping enthusiasts

Personas

From the list of archetypes, we created 3 specific user personas. A persona is used to show the ideal user. For our test, we primarily focused on hiking enthusiasts.

Testing

The testing form can be found here.

  • Pre-test questions

    Since we were pooling from a large number of individuals for our testing. A pre-test questionnaire was used to allow us to find what type of user they were and how they related to our personas.



  • Usability Testing

    • Preference Test

      First we presented our users with 2 pages. One of our page, and one of its closest competitor. Users were asked to choose a preferred design between 2 pages.


    • 5 Second Test

      We then presented our users with a screen from our website. Here, our users are shown the screen for 5 seconds, and must answer questions about that screen.


    • First Click Test

      Finally, we again presented our users with another screen from our page, where they are asked where they would click to complete a given task.



  • Treelly Test

    The tree test was focused solely on the navigation menu of the website. It allowed us to look into how our users think when moving through a website.



  • Think Aloud Tasks

    We then presented our users with our think aloud tests. This is where users are given tasks and are asked to describe their thought process whilst completing it. Whether it be confusion, layout issues, design flaws, we encouraged them to speak their minds and made note of their comments.



  • System Usability Scale

    Once our users completed each task, we asked them follow up questions to rate it's difficulty on a scale of 1-10. Their responses were then inputted into our System Usability Scale (SUS), which displays the weight of tasks in terms of their importance and severity. Through the scale we were able to weigh the importance of each task to determine which tasks needed the most attention.

Results

  • Usability Testing

    • Preference Test

      For our preference test we compared the landing page of MEC with one of its competitors, Atmosphere.

      We found from our test that 75% of our users preferred the MEC landing page. The reasons given to us were:

      • I like the colours on the banner more
      • It’s more bright in colour and more concise
      • Easier to scan navigation
      • Less visual noise

    • 5 Second Test

      For our 5 second test, we presented our users with the page for camping equipment and asked them the prompt: "What was the first thing you noticed on the page?".

      The feedback we got included:

      • Camping gear
      • Camp stoves
      • Product image layout, side navigation for categories
      • Stoves
      • Products in the middle of the page

      We then asked our users "Did you notice the Stove Finder?", and found that only 50% of our participants noticed it.


    • First Click Test

      Finally with our first click test, we provided our users with the jackets page and gave them the prompt "Where would you click to find the cheapest items?".

      From the heat map we can see that 75% of our users realized to click the 'sort by' button, while the 25% clicked elsewhere. We then asked our users "What type of sort options did you expect to see?" to which we got the following feedback:

      • Filter by price
      • A filter list, that includes price lowest to highest
      • Lowest to highest in $$$
      • Sort by cheapest to most expensive; vice versa. Best Reviewed. Newest products

      We followed that question with "How often do you use a sort or filter function?", and found a mixed response within our participants.

Solutions

PXL Prioritization Framework

Based on the comments we recieved from our participants we tested, we developed a list of potential solutions to their pain points. To ensure we are prioritizing the most relevant issues first.


The PXL Prioritization Framework uses a binary scale find out the most important issues based on ease of implementation and impact.


Once we had our results, we decided on the 3 that would be the most impactful.


Recommendation 1

During the first task, only 50% of our participants used the compare function because they first clicked into the cart page and did not see it there. For the participants that used the compare function, they had to click back to each individual item and add it to their compare list.


Our recommendation to fix this issue would be to implement the compare function into the cart page. This will allow users to compare all the items currently in their cart, without having to go back to the product pages to add items to their compare list.

Recommendation 2

One issue that we found was that after a user makes an account and signs in, they still need to make a membership account and add the “membership” card to their cart. This process was confusing because they needed to have a user account and then another membership account in order to make a purchase.


Our solution to this was to combine the two processes together, so that when you make an account, you also begin making a membership. On the first page you would enter an email and password, and when you click next, the user is prompted to enter the rest if their details to create the membership.

Recommendation 3

During our testing process, we found that when users wanted to click into an individual item to view more information, it brought them to a whole new page. In order to return to the original page, they either had to use the browers return page button or use the breadcrumbs.


To fix this issue, we decided to add quick view function. On this quick view pop-up, you can see what sizes are avaliable and the different colours. This makes it easy to return to the original items page without having to use the browsers return page function.
